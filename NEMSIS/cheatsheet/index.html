Introduction

Project Overview

The healthcare landscape is ever-evolving, and in this wave of advancements, predicting patient outcomes has gained unparalleled importance. Critical Care Doctors at MaineHealth aim to harness data insights from cardiac arrest patients in the ICU to pinpoint determinants that influence either good or poor outcomes. A key resource for this investigation is the 2020 National Emergency Medical System (NEMSIS) database, which is a vast repository of nationwide 911 calls. With the dataset's breadth, our primary focus will be a sampled version that zeroes in on cardiac arrest cases to delve into the nuances of "Urbanicity" and its implications.

Approach

The core objective of this project is to impute the EMS data from the National Emergency Medical Services Information System (NEMSIS) in order to find predictors that can forecast cardiac arrest outcomes in ICU patients by urbanicity/rurality. More specifically:

  • Appraise different data imputation methodologies, primarily focusing on MICE and MissForest.
  • Set the groundwork for subsequent modeling of prediction of cardiac arrest patients' outcomes are good or poor.

There's the potential challenge of data granularity, missing data points, and the intricacies of dealing with real-world clinical data. Another risk factor is ensuring that data interpretations align with clinical realities. For example:

  • EMS records data from different patients at different intervals. For some patients, it may be possible to record every minute, while for others it may be every hour. This inconsistency in data granularity can make it difficult to perform time series analysis or compare trends between patients.
  • In tracking patient symptoms and data sets, some entries may be missing key symptoms. This lack of information may be critical, especially if these symptoms are important predictors of patient outcome.

Besides the established methodologies, we propose to integrate data imputation techniques to better manage gaps in data. This approach is hoped to enhance the dataset's integrity and the veracity of our findings. Primary focus on:

  • Manual Imputation
  • Model Imputation

Data

Data Overview

The National Emergency Medical System (NEMSIS) dataset is a crucial resource in our project, providing expansive insights into emergency medical services across the United States. Managed by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, this comprehensive database details nationwide 911 emergency calls, encompassing various aspects of pre-hospital care. Key highlights of the dataset include:

  • Cardiac Arrest Focus: Our analysis centers on a subset of NEMSIS, specifically targeting cardiac arrest cases. This allows for a focused study on critical care outcomes in the ICU.
  • Rich Data Points: The dataset includes diverse information such as patient demographics, details of the medical emergency, EMS interventions, and subsequent patient outcomes.
  • "Urbanicity" Exploration: A significant part of our analysis is understanding the impact of geographic and demographic factors, particularly in rural versus urban contexts, on cardiac arrest outcomes.
  • Original data source: NEMSIS Database.
  • For more information about EMS Dataset, please go to NEMSIS v3 EMSDataSet Guide .

Data Structure

The original EMS Dataset is filtered out by the NEMSIS Case Definition of Cardiac Arrest. Our dataset selects 440k patients out of 44 million patients. The dataset includes both continuous and categorical variables. We primary focus on these datasets:

  • ComputedElements_CA: The ComputedElements_CA.csv includes the region, age, and EMS information of patients, the dataset contains 448683 rows.
  • Pub_PCRevents_CA: The Pub_PCREvents_CA.csv includes the patient's personal information and some medical records. The dataset contains 448683 rows
  • FACTPCRPRIMARYSYMPTOM_CA: The FACTPCRPRIMARYSYMPTOM_CA.csv contains the primary sign and symptom present in the patient or observed by EMS personnel. The dataset contains 438358 rows.
  • FACTPCRADDITIONALSYMPTOM_CA: The FACTPCRADDITIONALSYMPTOM_CA.csv contains other symptoms identified by the patient or observed by EMS personnel. The dataset contains 521362 rows.
  • FACTPCRPRIMARYIMPRESSION_CA: The FACTPCRPRIMARYIMPRESSION_CA.csv contains the EMS personnel's impression of the patient's primary problem or most significant condition which led to the management given to the patient (treatments, medications, or procedures). The dataset contains 429932 rows.
  • FACTPCRSECONDARYIMPRESSION_CA: The FACTPCRSECONDARYIMPRESSION_CA.csv contains The EMS personnel's impression of the patient's secondary problem or most significant condition which led to the management given to the patient (treatments, medications, or procedures). The dataset contains 446186 rows.

Manual Imputation

ePatient_13:

  • Method: Missing values were filled with a mixture of two categories ('9906003' and '9906001') in a 6:4 proportion.
  • Reason: This method was chosen to maintain the existing distribution of gender. It's implied that these proportions reflect the characteristic balance within the dataset.

ePatient_16, eScene_06, eScene_07:

  • Method: Missing values in these columns were filled with their respective mode.
  • Reason: The mode was used for imputation as it's a simple and effective way to maintain the existing distribution for categorical variables, particularly when the most common value is significantly more frequent than others.

eResponse_07:

  • Method: Similar to ePatient_16, eScene_06, and eScene_07, missing values were filled with the mode.
  • Reason: Given the very low percentage of missing values (only 0.009584%), filling in with the mode is a straightforward choice that minimally impacts the overall distribution of the data.

eSituation_07 and eSituation_08:

  • Method: Missing values were filled proportionally based on the first and second modes. The filling was done such that the proportion of these two values in the imputed data matched their existing proportion in the non-missing data.
  • This approach was chosen to maintain the original distribution of the top two categories in these columns. It's particularly useful when the top two categories dominate the distribution.

MICE

Consider the large features and the potential relationship between each column, the Bayesian ridge regression was used as an estimator of MICE. And after ten iterations, the model reached convergence.

  • Targeted at Continuous Data: In our dataset, MICE is applied specifically to continuous columns. These columns likely have intricate relationships with each other, where MICE's regression-based approach can effectively estimate missing values.
  • Iterative Updating: The method iteratively updates missing values in these continuous columns, leveraging the interdependencies among them. This is particularly beneficial given that dataset may contain complex, non-linear relationships.
  • Preserving Data Integrity: Given the nature of the dataset, MICE helps in preserving the statistical properties of the continuous variables, which is crucial for any subsequent analysis or modeling.

Miss Forest

The Random Forest was used as an estimator of Miss Forest.The tolerance(ratio of changing values) was set for the function, and the model reached convergence at second iteration.

  • Focused on Categorical Data: Miss Forest was used to impute missing values in categorical columns. This choice is pertinent because Miss Forest excels in dealing with mixed-type data.
  • Random Forest Advantage: Leveraging the Random Forest algorithm, Miss Forest treats each missing value as a prediction problem, utilizing the other variables in the dataset as predictors. This is especially useful in the dataset where categorical variables might have complex relationships with other variables.
  • Capturing Complex Patterns: For the categorical variables in the dataset, capturing complex patterns and relationships is essential for accurate imputation.
